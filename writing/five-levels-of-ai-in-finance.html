The five levels of AI in finance

Most operators stop at the third. The jump that compounds is from automating tasks to encoding judgment — with the controls a CFO would demand.

Ashwin Kumar · 8 July 2026

Every finance team now has someone who "uses AI." Almost none of them can put its output in front of a board without rewriting it first. The gap between those two states is the whole game — and it has levels.

I have spent two years rebuilding how a finance function runs on AI. Not by buying a tool, and not by writing better prompts, but by building an operating model where AI does the volume and the judgment stays with me. Here is the ladder I use to describe it, and the one rung that actually matters.

The ladder

  • 1
    Ask
    Questions to a chatbot when you're stuck. Useful, occasional, nothing compounds.
  • 2
    Draft
    AI writes the first version, you clean it up. The ceiling is still you, from scratch.
  • 3
    Template
    A library of saved prompts for repeat tasks. Where most strong operators stop.
  • 4
    Automate
    Custom tools that do real work end to end, without you in the loop each time.
  • 5
    Compound
    Judgment encoded as rules; every correction captured so it never recurs; system and operator sharpen together each cycle.

Levels 1 through 3 feel like progress and mostly aren't. They make you faster at the tasks you already did, but the ceiling never moves — it is still your time, your attention, your willingness to re-explain the same context every morning. A prompt library is the comfortable plateau. It is genuinely useful, which is exactly why so many capable people stop there and call it transformation.

The jump that compounds is not from Level 3 to Level 4. It is from doing tasks to encoding judgment.

Why Level 3 is a trap

A saved prompt captures what you asked. It does not capture why you rejected the first answer. When you tell the system "this variance is correlation, not cause — don't write it that way," a template forgets that by tomorrow. You will make the same correction next month, and the month after. The work gets faster; the thinking never accumulates.

Level 4 — real automation — is better, but on its own it is brittle. A tool that runs end to end without you will confidently produce a wrong answer at scale, which is worse than a slow right one. Automation without encoded judgment is how AI ends up putting an unsourced number in front of people who will act on it.

What Level 5 actually is

The engine isn't the model. It's the loop. Four steps, and three of them are mine:

Define — I set the ask and the standard before anything is written. Draft — the system produces at speed, and tags every figure with its source and as-of date, or flags what it can't source rather than guessing. Refine — I correct the reasoning, not just the wording. Encode — the correction becomes a standing rule, dated and reversible, applied automatically from then on.

Corrections work like case law. Anything I've had to say three times becomes permanent. The same mistake doesn't come back, and each cycle starts ahead of the last.

That is the difference between a faster analyst and a compounding system. The analyst improves while they're paying attention. The system improves whether I'm paying attention or not, because the improvement is written down.

The part a CFO cares about

None of this matters if you can't trust the output. The reason most AI never reaches a boardroom is that it can't show its work. So the discipline runs the other way: every number carries its source and its as-of date, or it doesn't ship — never a guess someone could act on. Anything headed for a board self-grades against a fixed bar and gets rewritten until it clears. That verification layer is the part most AI skips, and the only part a CFO actually cares about.

What it looks like in practice

Take the quarterly board update package — a task that repeats each quarter. Zoom into one component, the variance commentary: I define the bar: every variance over threshold gets a driver, an owner, and a next action, and volume effects are separated from rate effects. The system drafts the commentary line by line with sources attached. I refine — this cycle, it named a correlation as a driver and double-counted two effects; I fix the reasoning. Then I encode both fixes as standing rules: always split volume versus rate, never attribute a driver without a supporting cut.

What used to take two analysts most of a week is now a same-day first draft that already carries its sources. Next quarter, this cycle's two corrections are already baked in. That is the full loop on one task — and the same discipline now runs across the function.
