Corrections as case law

How encoding every fix as a dated, reversible rule turns a fast assistant into a system that compounds.

Ashwin Kumar · 23 July 2026 · Companion to "The five levels" and "The Level 3 trap"

The first time an AI tool gets a number wrong, that is the model's failure. The second time it makes the same mistake, the failure is yours. The correction you gave it last month lived in your head, so this month you are giving it again. Speed without memory is just faster repetition.

There is a discipline that fixes this, and it is older than software. It is how common law works.

How a body of law gets smarter

Common law was never written top to bottom. It compounds case by case. A judge rules, the ruling is recorded with its date and its reasoning, and it binds the cases that follow. In 1932 a woman said she found a snail in her bottle of ginger beer, and the ruling her case produced — that a manufacturer owes a duty of care to whoever ends up with the product — still decides cases nearly a century later. Nobody re-argues it. It is dated, reasoned, and citable, so every court since starts from it instead of starting over.

Precedent is not frozen, either. Later rulings narrow or overturn earlier ones, which is exactly why one of the three tests below is that a rule stays reversible. A finance function running on AI can work the same way, if you treat each correction as a ruling instead of a one-off.

What makes a correction compound

A fix only compounds if it has three properties. Miss one and it decays back into folklore: a thing people used to know.

1
Dated

It carries the day it took effect. You can see when the rule entered force and revisit it when the world changes, instead of obeying a reason no one in the room remembers.

2
Reversible

It is a rule, not a scar. When the reason expires you repeal it cleanly, with a record of why it existed and why it went. A system you cannot unwind is a system you stop trusting.

3
Sourced

It points back to the specific miss that caused it, so you can always answer the only question that matters a year later: why does this rule exist?

A template remembers what you asked. Case law remembers what you decided, and why. One saves keystrokes; the other saves judgment.

Two corrections, made concrete

That is abstract until you watch it happen. Here are the two mistakes an AI draft makes most often when it explains why a number moved, and the rule each one becomes. The numbers are made up; the mistakes are not.

Correction 1 · "sold more" is not "charged more"

A product line did $1,000 last quarter and $1,200 this quarter, up 20%. The draft writes: "Revenue grew 20% on stronger pricing." It reads fine. It is wrong. The price never moved: you sold 120 units instead of 100, at the same $10 each. Every dollar of the gain was volume; none was price. Tell the board "we raised prices" and they will plan around pricing power that does not exist. The ruling: split every change into volume (sold more) and rate (charged more), and never call one the other.

Correction 2 · no "because" without the data behind it

Next the draft writes: "Margins fell because freight costs rose." Also plausible. Nobody checked. Pull the numbers and freight barely moved; the real cause was a one-time discount to a single large customer. The ruling: no cause without its cut. A cut is the slice of data where the cause is visibly doing the damage — pull margin for that one account and the whole drop appears. A story that cannot produce its cut does not reach the board.

Why the accumulation is the asset

One correction saves an hour, once. A hundred of them are a different asset entirely. The next person to do the job (or the next model) inherits on day one what used to take a year of getting it wrong. The knowledge stops walking out the door at six o'clock.

The failure mode

The alternative is the world most teams live in. Corrections stay in one person's memory, or die as a one-time "let's not do that again." Nothing is written, so everything is re-argued. The same issue returns every quarter; every new joiner steps on the same rake. Effort repeats. Learning does not. It feels like work, because it is. It just never adds up to a system.

This is the whole of the top of the ladder in one sentence: not automating the task, but encoding the judgment, so the system carries your standard and not just your labour. The assistant forgets overnight. The case law does not. That is the difference between a tool you drive and a system that compounds while you sleep.

The way in is unglamorous. Pick one task you repeat. Write down the three corrections you currently make from memory, each dated and pointing at the miss that caused it. Make the system read them before the next run. That is the whole method, on one task.

Where does your team keep its corrections — in a system, or in someone's head? If you lead finance or operations and you are moving AI from pilot to real cadence, that is exactly the conversation I want to have.
